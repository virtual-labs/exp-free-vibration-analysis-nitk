Free Vibration Analysis of 2 DOF Undamped System

A two degree of freedom system can be defined as a system, that requires two independent variables to describe its position from the equilibrium. It has two equations of motion i.e., for two masses or for each degree of freedom. It introduces the coupling phenomenon where the motion of any one of the two independent coordinates is dependent on the other, through the coupling springs or dash-pots. Consider an ideal two degrees of freedom system as shown in Fig 1, where the masses are constrained to move in the direction of the spring axes. Let x1x_1x1​ and x2x_2x2​ be the displacements of the masses at any instant measured from the equilibrium positions of these masses respectively, which are positive in the direction shown. Let’s assume X2X_2X2​ > X1X_1X1​.

Then the spring forces acting on these masses are shown below, giving us the following differential equations:

m1x¨_1+k1x1+k2(x1−x2)=0m_1 \ddot{x}\_1 + k_1 x_1 + k_2 (x_1 - x_2) = 0m1​x¨_1+k1​x1​+k2​(x1​−x2​)=0

m2x¨_2+k2(x2−x1)=0m_2 \ddot{x}\_2 + k_2 (x_2 - x_1) = 0m2​x¨_2+k2​(x2​−x1​)=0


On re-arranging the equations,

m1x¨1+(k1+k2)x1=k2x2m_1 \ddot{x}_1 + (k_1 + k_2) x_1 = k_2 x_2 m1​x¨1​+(k1​+k2​)x1​=k2​x2​

m2x¨2+k2x2=k2x1m_2 \ddot{x}_2 + k_2 x_2 = k_2 x_1m2​x¨2​+k2​x2​=k2​x1​

Assuming the solutions for x1x_1x1​ and x2x_2x2​ to be harmonic in nature under steady state conditions.

When mass m1m_1m1​ and m2m_2m2​ executes harmonic vibration at frequency ω\omegaω,

x1=X1sin⁡(ωt)x_1 = X_1 \sin(\omega t)x1​=X1​sin(ωt)
x2=X2sin⁡(ωt)x_2 = X_2 \sin(\omega t)x2​=X2​sin(ωt)

Substituting the above equation in the differential equations and cancelling out the common terms sin⁡(ωt)\sin(\omega t)sin(ωt).

[−m1ω2+(k1+k2)]X1=k2X2[-m_1 \omega^2 + (k_1 + k_2)] X_1 = k_2 X_2[−m1​ω2+(k1​+k2​)]X1​=k2​X2​
[−m2ω2+k2]X2=k2X1[-m_2 \omega^2 + k_2] X_2 = k_2 X_1[−m2​ω2+k2​]X2​=k2​X1​

These equations will give the following two equations respectively,

X1X2=k2[(k1+k2)−m1ω2]\frac{X_1}{X_2} = \frac{k_2}{[(k_1 + k_2) - m_1 \omega^2]}X2​X1​​=[(k1​+k2​)−m1​ω2]k2​​
X1X2=[k2−m2ω2]k2\frac{X_1}{X_2} = \frac{[k_2 - m_2 \omega^2]}{k_2}X2​X1​​=k2​[k2​−m2​ω2]​


From the above two equations, we have

k2[(k1+k2)−m1ω2]=[k2−m2ω2]k2\frac{k_2}{[(k_1 + k_2) - m_1 \omega^2]} = \frac{[k_2 - m_2 \omega^2]}{k_2}[(k1​+k2​)−m1​ω2]k2​​=k2​[k2​−m2​ω2]​


On Solving,

[(k1+k2)−m1ω2][k2−m2ω2]=(k2)2[(k_1 + k_2) - m_1 \omega^2][k_2 - m_2 \omega^2] = (k_2)^2[(k1​+k2​)−m1​ω2][k2​−m2​ω2]=(k2​)2

Or,

m1m2ω4−[m1k2+m2(k1+k2)]ω2+k1k2=0m_1 m_2 \omega^4 - [m_1 k_2 + m_2 (k_1 + k_2)] \omega^2 + k_1 k_2 = 0m1​m2​ω4−[m1​k2​+m2​(k1​+k2​)]ω2+k1​k2​=0

The above equation is quadratic in ω2\omega^2ω2 and gives two values of ω2\omega^2ω2, and therefore two positive values of ω\omegaω corresponding to two natural frequencies ωn1\omega_{n1}ωn1​ and ωn2\omega_{n2}ωn2​ of the system. The above equation is called the frequency equation since the roots of this equation gives the natural frequencies of the system.

The amplitude ratio of two equations,

k2[(k1+k2)−m1ω2]=[k2−m2ω2]k2\frac{k_2}{[(k_1 + k_2) - m_1 \omega^2]} = \frac{[k_2 - m_2 \omega^2]}{k_2}[(k1​+k2​)−m1​ω2]k2​​=k2​[k2​−m2​ω2]​

If in either of the equations, on substituting for ω\omegaω the value of ωn1\omega_{n1}ωn1​ , we have the first mode shape of the system i.e., when the system is vibrating with the first natural frequency ωn1\omega_{n1}ωn1​, the mode shape is such that the ratio of the amplitude of two masses is equal to the solution of above equation.

Similarly, in either of the equations, substituting for ω\omegaω the value of ωn2\omega_{n2}ωn2​, we have the second mode shape of the system i.e., when the system is vibrating with the second natural frequency ωn2\omega_{n2}ωn2​, the mode shape is such that the ratio of the amplitude of two masses is equal to the solution of above equation.

Under certain condition, at any point, the system may execute harmonic vibrations at any of the two natural frequencies, and these are known as the principal modes of vibration. A system having two degrees of freedom can vibrate in two principal modes of vibrations corresponding to its two natural frequencies, the mode shapes being given by either of the equations after substituting for ω\omegaω the corresponding value of natural frequency.
